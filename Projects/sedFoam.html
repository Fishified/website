Objectives:

1. Answer the question: Is sedFoam accurate at simultaneously modeling 3D bedload applications?

2. Determine if the model can predict the temporal evolution of the 3D bathymetry of the scouring bed.

3. Verify the coupling occurring between the continously scouring sediment bed and the overlying hydrodynamic flow field.

4. Evaluate the fidelity of sedFoam to replicate the 3D deposition pattern in a downstream sediment trap.

Publication details:

The results of this work will appear as a publication in 2020.

A recently developed OpenFOAM solver sedFoam is validated for its ability to replicate an extensive set of experimental data. In contrast to more traditional approaches to 3D sediment modeling, sedFoam solves both the water and sediment as independent phases (a two phase flow), with a continuity and momentum equation for each phase. The field parameters specific to each phase (i.e., their velocity field) are however strongly coupled. This means that the movement of one phase will invariably cause movement in the other phase. This so called Euler-Euler approach (i.e., each field is considered within a Eulerian framework) is relatively new in the field of sediment modeling. The method is attractive over its predecessors, principally because the Euler-Euler approach can account for virtually all the possible inter-phase forces involved in a sediment-fluid system (e.g., drag, virtual mass, lateral lift, intergranular stresses). Therefore, few if any, empirical assumptions are required - meaning only a comprehensive description of the fundamental physics describing sediment-fluid interactions are all that are required.

This work was performed in the Hydraulics laboratory at the Universit√© de Sherbrooke in a controlled laboratory flume. A flow was applied over an initially static bed of approximately uniform sediment grains. A thin layer of sediment quickly mobilizes due to the shear stress applied by the flowing water. The mobilized sediment grains are deposited and collected in a trap downstream. With time, a 3D scour pattern develops on the bed and a 3D deposition pattern forms in the downstream trap. A variety of experimental methods were applied to provide experimental data for model validation. These methods include:

Multi-particle ultra-violet fluorescent particle tracking to estimate the velocity field of the mobilized bed layer.

Multi-plane stereoscopic particle image velocimetry to reconstruct the time-averaged initial conditions of the water phase.

High-density 3D photogrammetric reconstructions of the erosion and deposition patterns.

Instantaneous short duration sPIV measurements of the coupled hydrodynamic and sediment flow fields.

Empirical sediment flux measurements.

Improving fish passage through culverts with baffles

Fig. 1: Rendered image of the experimental flume used to perform this study. The ABiKiS flume (Aquatic Biomechanics and Kinematic Research Station) is situated at the United States Geological Survey-Leetown Science Center in Turners Falls, Massachusetts. Fish were placed in the downstream staging area and allowed to volitionally make attempts up the flume during the trial period.

Fig. 2: The three different baffle configurations tested. Trials of the bare flume were also performed. The most spatially dense spoiler baffle configuration is depicted on the left, the full-width weir baffle in the center and the lower density spoiler baffle configuration on the right.

Fig. 3: Maximum distances of ascent for the various baffle conditions, species of trout and flow rates tested. The bare flume is seen to be the least effective at improve the maximum distances of ascent. The weir baffles, in contrast, allow even the smallest fish tested to reach the upper reach of the test section.

Fig. 4: View of the flume looking downstream towards the staging area. The mirrors were put into place to capture ventral views of the ascending trout. Each mirror was dedicated a Raspberry Pi to record the ascending fish over the trial duration.

Fig. 5: View of the flume looking upstream with the weir baffles in place. The flow rapidly attains its normal depth downstream of the hydraulic jump that occurs over the hydraulically rougher section where the weir baffles were installed.

Fig. 6: Statistically derived survival curves showing the proportion of attempting trout expected to attain a given distance over the test section. In general, the bare flume was the most challenging, smaller fish presented more difficulty, smaller fish benefited most from the weir baffles, slight improvements were made with a denser spoiler baffle configuration and brown trout demonstrated lower maximum distances of ascent than brook trout.

Objectives:

1. Investigate how various baffle configurations improve the maximum distances of ascent of trout attempting passage over an exterior flume bed simulating a smooth-bottomed culvert.

2. Understand the influence that species, body-length and flow rate have on maximum distances of ascent.

Publication details:

Duguay, J.M. and Lacey, R.W.J. and Castro-Santos, T. (2018). Influence of baffles on upstream passage of brook trout and brown trout in an experimental box culvert. Canadian Journal of Fisheries and Aquatic Sciences, 76, 28-41.

The experimental work of this study was done at the United States Geological Survey-Leetown Science Center in their outdoor Aquatic Biomechanics and Kinematic Research Station (ABiKiS). The motivation for the study was to continue improving our understanding of how various baffle forms can improve upstream passage of trout through roadway culverts. The results show that not all baffles are equal, and that fish passage success over each treatment of baffles is highly subject to the species and life-stage of the trout.

Instrumentation, methods and software used:

  • 35 m long (we only used 15 m), 0.64 m wide flume (ABiKiS)
  • Wild brook (Salvelinus fontinalis) and brown trout (Salmo trutta)
  • Multi-antenna TIRIS radio frequency identification system
  • pyTIRIS written in Python (simplifies raw TIRIS data analysis)
  • R Studio for statistical analysis

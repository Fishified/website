Towards understanding how sediment affects fish passage through baffled culverts

Objectives:

1. Answer the question: Does sediment in baffled culverts reduce the benefit baffles provide to fish passage?

2. Understand how fish interact with complex turbulent recirculatory zones.

3. Relate instantaneous 3D fish trajectories to time-averaged 3D flow fields.

Publication details:

Duguay, J.M. and Foster, B. and Lacey, R.W.J. and Castro-Santos, T. (2018). Sediment infilling benefits rainbow trout passage in a baffled channel. Ecological Engineering, 125, 38-49.

This work provides insights on how fish interact with low-velocity recirculatory zones common to engineered fish passage structures. Such zones regularily appear downstream of bed-mounted objects as swift currents sweep above them. It is largely held that fish energeticaly benefit from these zones by actively seeking them to take refuge within them from the high velocity oncoming flow. In this study we used a combination of high-speed 3D video tracking of juvenile rainbow trout navigating an experimental flume fitted with a number of bed-mounted 'baffles' intended to improve passage performance. In a second condition, wedges of fixed sediment were placed in the lee of the bar to obstruct the formation of the recirculatory zone. What found the sediment actually improved passage performance across a number of metrics.

Without the sediment wedges in place, the fish were observed to be entrained into the turbulent recirculatory zone. The predominantly upstream directed flow within the recirculatory zone inhibited fish from holding position and, in many cases ultimately caused them to abandon their upstream attempt. By filling the recirculatory zone with sediment, passage performance was improved, because the sediment wedge eliminated the counter-current recirculatory flow thus permitting fish to better maintain postural stability while navigating upstream. This study provided the proof of concept needed to continue further research towards investigating how species exhibiting different body morphologies, spanning the range of life-cycle stages, interact with larger scale recirculatory zones commonly encountered in rivers, streams and fishways.


Profile views of juvenile rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) navigating a bed-mounted bar (1st video) and the same bar with sediment obstructing the recirculation zone it the bar's lee (2nd video). The third video shows the reconstructed 3D trajectory of the fish in the 1st video. Average cross-sectional velocity in flume is 0.75 m/s, however just above the baffle it can be in excess of 1.2 m/s. Video was taken at 300 Hz and therefore the fish swims much faster in real-time.
